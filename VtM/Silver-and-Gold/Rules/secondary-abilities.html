Secondary Abilities

Secondary Abilities are specializations of Primary Abilities (Talents, Skills, and Knowledges). They are acquired the same way as primary abilities, with the following notes:

  • At character creation, they cost 2 Freebie points per dot, the same as other abilities.
  • During the game, they cost 2 XP to acquire a new ability. Each additional dot costs the Current Rating in XP.
    In addition to being cheaper to acquire than Primary Abilities, Secondary Abilities can be used in two ways:
  1. As a substitute for a Primary Ability when appropriate. For example, a character who lacks any dots in Survival but has the Secondary Skill of Tracking may use their Tracking rating, with no penalties, on a roll involving tracking (usually Intelligence or Resolve + Survival).
  2. In addition to the related Primary, or, with the GM's approval, with any other Primary. For example, a person with Athletics 3 and Skiing 2 is attempting to ski down a slope; they may add their Skiing rating to the Athletics one for a total of 5 Dice (plus the appropriate Trait, of course). Question: How do secondary abilities, like Public Speaking, work, and why would I take them?

Public Speaking, a Secondary Talent, is a subset of the Primary Talent, Expression. Here's the explanation for Expression from the V20 rulebook: 

This is your ability to get your point across clearly, whether through conversation, poetry, or even in 140 characters or fewer. Characters with high Expression can phrase their opinions or beliefs in a manner that cannot be ignored (even if their opinions are misinformed or worthless). They might also be talented actors, skilled at conveying moods or communicating emotion with every gesture. Additionally, this Talent represents your ability for poetry, creative writing, or other literary art forms. For many elders, Expression is the subtle art of crafting a satirical epigram capable of socially crippling oneâ€™s longtime rival. For younger Kindred, Expression may well be the key to convincing thirty stake-wielding Anarchs to converge on the Sheriffâ€™s private hunting ground with the right text message.

As you know, if you have four or more dots in any trait, you can specialize that trait. You might wonder what the difference is between specializing a primary ability, like Expression, with taking a secondary ability like Public Speaking. There are three main differences:

  • You generally get no benefit from specializing a trait until you have at least four dots in that trait; a secondary ability is useful even with a single dot.
  • Having a specialization means that you can reroll any 10s (keeping the successes) whenever doing something with that specialization; having a secondary ability means that add both your primary and secondary ability dice to your pool.
  • It is cheaper in experience points to raise secondary abilities than it is to raise primary abilities.

Question: Does this mean I can potentially have a Dice Pool that exceeds 10?

Yes. For example, if you have 5 dots in Athletics and 5 dots in Climbing, and a 5 DEX character with Celerity 5 is climbing Mt. Everest, you would have a 20 dice pool. Note that even if you burned blood to raise your DEX even higher, it is not possible for anyone with power less than an Elder vampire to have more than 20 dice in a pool. If that seems too high, consider that even without this Secondary Abilities, you can have an 18 dice pool. For example, if you have DEX 5, Celerity 5, and Athletics 5, you can burn 3 blood to bring your DEX up to 8. So this really isn't that much more powerful. If you're willing to spend the XP, you can have the dice!

 

Here's an example of what I mean. Let's look at two characters, Jack and Jill, who have 15 XP to spend. Both of them spend 3 XP to start with 1 dot in the Primary Talent, Expression. Jack spends his XP to raise the number of dots he has in Expression, and Jill instead takes the Secondary Ability "Public Speaking" and uses her XP here. 

As noted, Jack spends 3 XP to acquire Expression. It will cost him (the current rating x 2) to raise it, so to raise it from 1 dot to 2 dots costs 2 XP. To raise it from 2 to 3 costs 4 XP. To raise it to 4 dots will cost him 6 XP. At 4 dots, he can specialize the Talent, and he does, taking the Specialization "Public Speaking." Total cost, 15 XP.

Also as noted, Jill spends 3 XP to acquire 1 dot in Expression, and then she spends 2 XP to acquire Public Speaking. It costs her (the current rating) to raise it, so going from 1 dot to 2 costs only 1 XP. 2 to 3 costs 2 XP, and 3 to 4 costs 3 XP. At 4 dots, she can also take a specialization, and she does, "Impromptu Speeches." Total cost, 11 XP.  She chooses to spend the remaining 4 XP elsewhere.

During the game, Jack and Jill are both giving speeches to an angry crowd and trying to calm them down. The GM says the roll is Charisma + Expression, Difficulty 8, and they will need a complete success (3 successes) to keep the crowd from killing them. Jill asks if she can add her Secondary Talent of Public Speaking to the roll, and the GM agrees. That means that Jack has 7 dice in his pool (Charisma + Expression) and Jill has 8 dice (Charisma + Expression  + Public Speaking). For the sake of this example, they roll identically (they both have 3 Charisma), except that Jill gets one more die added to her roll.
(7+5+10+9+5+1+6) and Jill's extra die is an 8. 
For Jack, this gives him 1 success (Difficulty 8, the 9 and 10 are successes but the 1 cancels one of them out). He points out that he rolled a 10 and he specializes in Public Speaking, which means he gets to reroll the 10 (keeping that success) and trying to get an additional success. He rolls a "8" and fails. Jack has only 2 successes; the crowd is hesitating but isn't quite swayed.
For Jill, this same roll gives her 2 successes. She points out that this speech was improvised and that she has a specialization (in her secondary ability) of "impromptu speeches;" thus, she also gets to reroll the 10, and this time that same "8" is an additional success, giving her 3 successes total. The crowd chills out, and Jill is safe!

Sounds great, right? Okay, but the very next night, the Prince wants our characters to write a speech for him. This time, the roll is Intelligence + Expression, and the Difficulty is 6. It will take 5 successes to write a speech that makes the Prince truly happy. Both our characters have 3 Intelligence. Each roll takes an hour, and they have four hours to write the speech.
Jack gets to roll 7 Dice. Jill will only get to roll 4. Why? Because Jack's primary talent of Expression encompasses more than personally delivering a oratory; it includes most forms of literary art. Jill's secondary talent doesn't, so she only has that 1 dot in Expression to come to her aid.
